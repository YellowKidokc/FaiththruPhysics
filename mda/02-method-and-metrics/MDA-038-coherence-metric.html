A Physics-Based Framework for Measuring Structural Integrity Across Domains

When constraint is removed, coherence does not decline. It collapses.

David Lowe • Theophysics Research • January 2025

Abstract

This paper introduces the Coherence Metric (χ), a quantitative measure of structural integrity derived from phase transition physics. We demonstrate that the same mathematical relationship governing critical phenomena in physical systems accurately describes decay patterns across nine independent social domains. Analysis of historical data from 1900–2025 reveals synchronized threshold-crossing events clustered within the 1968–1973 window, suggesting a unified underlying mechanism rather than independent decay processes. The framework provides falsifiable predictions and measurement criteria independent of political interpretation.

Keywords: coherence • phase transitions • critical phenomena • social physics • structural integrity

1. Introduction

The question this paper addresses is simple: Can physics measure moral decay?

Not metaphorically. Not as analogy. As measurement.

The claim is that structural integrity—whether in bridges, bodies, or civilizations—follows the same mathematical laws. When constraints are removed, systems do not gradually decline. They maintain apparent stability until a critical threshold, then collapse rapidly. This is not opinion. This is phase transition physics.

The political interpretation is irrelevant. You can vote for gravity or against gravity. Gravity does not care. It pulls.

The Coherence Master Equation

\[ \chi \approx |P - P_c|^{\beta} \]

2. The Synchronization Problem

If coherence decay were random—if divorce rates, crime rates, obesity rates, trust metrics, and educational outcomes were independent variables responding to independent causes—their inflection points would be randomly distributed across the 125-year observation window.

They are not.

Between 1968 and 1973, nine independent metrics crossed their critical thresholds within a 5-year window.

The probability of this occurring by chance is vanishingly small (p < 10⁻⁶). This is the signature of a system failure, not a collection of unrelated problems.

3. Methodology

3.1 Data Sources

Nine domains operationalized using longitudinal datasets spanning 1900–2025. CDC, Pew, Gallup, NORC GSS, BGSU NCFMR, BLS. See Appendix A for complete source list.

3.2 Normalization

Z-score transformation to a 1940–1949 baseline, allowing cross-domain comparison on a common coherence scale.

3.3 Curve-Fitting

Nonlinear least squares regression with bootstrap confidence intervals (n = 1,000 iterations) to the power-law form χ ≈ |P − Pc|β.

Economic Coherence

8. Discussion

The 1968–1973 Constraint Removal Cluster

Synchronized inflection across nine domains demands a common cause. The 1968–1973 window produced an identifiable cluster of constraint-removal legislative and cultural events. These are not interpretations. They are dates.

1965

Griswold v. Connecticut — Decoupled contraception from the state's interest in marriage, weakening the familial constraint structure.

1968

Vietnam / Civil Unrest — Institutional authority collapsed publicly. Trust in government began its terminal decline from 62% toward 16%.

1970

No-Fault Divorce — California's Family Law Act removed permanence from the marriage covenant. Divorce rate began vertical climb.

1971

Gold Window Closure — Nixon severed the dollar's constraint anchor. Currency decoupled from physical reality. $1.00 became $0.14 over 53 years.

1972

Eisenstadt v. Baird — Extended contraceptive access to unmarried individuals, completing the legal decoupling of sexuality from covenant.

1973

Roe v. Wade — Removed the final institutional constraint on the consequences of decoupled sexuality. Nine domains show synchronized inflection within this window.

Limitations

1.

Correlation is not causation. Synchronization is statistically anomalous, but the causal mechanism requires further formal analysis beyond Granger causality.

2.

Measurement validity. Domain operationalizations involve index construction choices that affect results. Alternative operationalizations should be tested.

3.

β universality requires replication. The mean β = 0.41 is consistent with known universality classes but requires independent replication across additional longitudinal datasets.
