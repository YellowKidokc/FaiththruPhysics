Part 9A of 10: The Moral Decay of America

How the Amish Engineered a Membrane Against Entropy

David Lowe • Theophysics Institute

Most Americans look at the Amish and see nostalgia. A people frozen in 1850, opting out of modern life because they cannot or will not keep up. A museum exhibit you can drive past on the way to somewhere with reliable WiFi.

Run the equation on them and a different picture emerges.

The Amish are not behind. The Amish are running a different algorithm — a deliberately engineered, multi-generationally tuned system for manufacturing the one thing the rest of America has lost: coherence. Their unwritten code, the Ordnung, is not religious eccentricity. Strip the theological vocabulary and what remains is a working blueprint for a sociological membrane that filters information entropy while permitting matter and energy to cross.

The Amish have not rejected technology. They have engineered a filter that lets in everything that strengthens the local node and blocks everything that dissolves it.

This is part one of the Amish proof. We start with the architecture — the seven structural rules that keep the membrane intact — and the single axiom that generates them all.

The Technological Axiom of Distance

Standard modernity engineers technology to eliminate distance and friction. The goal is to make the far near and the difficult easy — to shrink the world to the size of a screen.

The Amish algorithm inverts this. It artificially imposes distance and friction so that interactions remain high-cost and therefore high-value. This is not Luddism. It is a coherence policy.

The intuition driving it: frictionless interaction produces frictionless abandonment. If the cost of leaving a relationship, a community, or a place is low enough, eventually that cost will be paid. The Ordnung holds the cost high on purpose.

The Axiom

The health of a social system is inversely proportional to the technological ease with which its members can bypass local interaction.

Every rule in the Ordnung is downstream of this single statement. Every technology evaluation reduces to one question: does adopting this lower the cost of bypassing the local node?

If yes, it is rejected — or modified until the answer becomes no.

The Coherence Metric: Inward vs. Outward Vectors

The variable the algorithm optimizes is the ratio of inward to outward attention vectors. Every technology, every habit, every institutional choice can be classified by which direction it pulls a person's attention, loyalty, and dependency.

Inward Vectors (Coherence)

Direct attention toward the local node — family, church district, immediate geography.

  • Horse and buggy
  • Pennsylvania Dutch dialect
  • Barn raising / mutual aid
  • One-room local school
  • Visiting as ritual practice

Outward Vectors (Entropy)

Pull attention toward the external world — state, global market, media ecosystem.

  • Television
  • The electric grid
  • The internet
  • Owned automobile
  • Public school

Maximize the inward column. Minimize the outward column. That is the entire policy. The Ordnung is a decades-tuned set of heuristics for keeping that ratio above the threshold at which a community can self-sustain across generations.

Silicon Valley discovered "attention economics" twenty years ago. The Amish have been running the operating system for three centuries.

The Seven Structural Rules

Here is what the algorithm actually does. Seven rules, each one a filter, each one targeting a specific failure mode that the modern world treats as a feature.

Rule 01

The Proximity Limit

Artifact: the horse and buggy. The horse imposes a biological speed limit (around 10 mph) and a working radius (about 20 miles). That radius defines the daily reality of the community. A church district can grow only as large as the number of families who can travel by horse to a single farmhouse for Sunday service. When it exceeds that limit, it divides — biological mitosis, not imperial expansion. The result: social density that aligns with Dunbar's number. You actually know your 150.

Rule 02

The Friction Requirement

Artifact: the pneumatic workshop. Amish craftsmen run power saws, sanders, and lathes — but driven by a noisy, fueled, locally maintained diesel air compressor rather than a silent invisible wall socket. Same output. Different source. The wall socket vanishes into the background; the compressor does not. The friction guarantees the technology remains a deliberate choice rather than ambient infrastructure. You cannot ignore a diesel engine. You can ignore a plug.

Rule 03

The Anti-Bypass Rule

Artifact: the 12-volt DC standard. The home runs on batteries, not the grid. Twelve-volt DC will power lights, fans, and basic tools — but it cannot easily run a television, a microwave, or a desktop PC without an inefficient inverter that drains the battery fast. This is not a limitation. It is a voltage firewall. The Amish home is structurally incompatible with the consumer electronics aisle at Walmart. The grid umbilical that feeds energy, media, and external authority into the modern home is severed at the wire.

Rule 04

The Sanctuary Boundary

Artifact: the telephone shanty. The phone is not forbidden. Its location is regulated. The phone lives in a small wooden shed at the end of the lane, often shared between families. Walking a quarter mile to make a call filters out trivial communication while leaving business and emergency channels intact. The home stays a sanctuary for face-to-face presence; the shanty contains the electronic interface. Some bishops even keep a fax machine in the shanty for business — the rule is about the threshold, not the device.

Rule 05

The Mutual Aid Imperative

Artifact: the absence of commercial insurance. Insurance is not banned for theological reasons. It is banned because it dissolves the bond. If a member can pay a distant corporation a premium and have their barn rebuilt without owing anyone in the community, the most powerful coherence ritual the community has — the barn raising — goes extinct in a generation. The risk of the individual is the bond of the group. Replace it with a contract and the bond evaporates.

Rule 06

The Finite Input Rule

Artifact: the propane tank, the battery, the fuel can. Resources must be metered — visibly depleting — not unmetered like the grid or the always-on internet. A blinking battery indicator and a propane gauge that reads "low" reattach the user to the physical limits of the world. The "infinite" supply is what produces the hedonic treadmill of consumer culture. Finiteness produces discipline. Conservative districts even regulate the capacity of solar arrays, because too much surplus power recreates the grid by other means.

Rule 07

The Ownership Distinction

Artifact: the "Amish taxi." Riding in cars is permitted. Owning one is forbidden. Critics call this hypocrisy. The algorithm calls it a brilliant filter. Owning a car restructures time, budget, and identity — it produces a sovereign individual who can leave at will. Hiring a car is expensive, requires planning, involves a third party, and leaves the user a tethered community member who is temporarily borrowing speed. Same vehicle. Completely different effect on the human being using it. Possession changes the habitus; access does not.

Why the Membrane Holds

Notice what the rules do collectively. The Proximity Limit caps the geographic radius. The Friction Requirement keeps technology in the foreground. The Anti-Bypass Rule severs the wire that would feed external authority into the home. The Sanctuary Boundary protects the family table. The Mutual Aid Imperative makes interdependence non-optional. The Finite Input Rule keeps the universe physical. The Ownership Distinction holds the line between using a tool and being changed by it.

Each rule, taken alone, looks quaint. Together they form a multi-layered membrane — redundant, mutually reinforcing, with no single point of failure. Remove any one rule and the others compensate. Remove three and the system starts to drift toward the surrounding entropy. Remove all seven and you have just described the suburban American household.

This is the engineering insight the modern world keeps missing. Coherence is not produced by a single heroic intervention. Coherence is produced by a stack of small, mutually reinforcing constraints applied consistently across centuries.

Inside the most aggressive information-entropy environment in human history — the surrounding internet age, with its smartphones, streaming, and algorithmic capture — the Amish membrane has held. The population doubles every twenty years. Most of the next generation, after exposure to the outside world during Rumspringa, chooses to come back and stay.

The mainstream American template is doing the opposite on every measurement that matters: fewer marriages, fewer children, weaker community ties, declining trust, declining religious participation, declining mental health. Same physics. Different membrane. Different result.

The Amish do not look like the past because they were left behind. They look like the past because they are running the only algorithm that has been empirically demonstrated to produce coherence at scale across multiple generations of accelerating entropy.
